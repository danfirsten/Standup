1\. Clear Product Framing (Sharpened)
=====================================

### One-sentence positioning (this matters a lot)

> **A persistent AI mentor that helps software engineers think clearly about feedback, impact, and growth—by remembering their context and turning messy emotions into concrete action.**

This is _not_:

*   “ChatGPT but for careers”
    
*   “An AI therapist”
    
*   “A generic coaching bot”
    

This _is_:

*   **A thinking partner for career meta-work**
    
*   **An interpreter of ambiguity**
    
*   **A long-term mirror for patterns engineers can’t see themselves**
    

If you get this right, users don’t come for answers—they come to _offload cognition_.

2\. The Core Insight You’re Building On (Very Strong)
=====================================================

Most engineers don’t fail because of:

*   Lack of intelligence
    
*   Lack of effort
    
*   Lack of technical skill
    

They struggle because:

*   Expectations are **implicit**
    
*   Feedback is **underspecified**
    
*   Success criteria are **political + contextual**
    
*   No one teaches the _mental models_ of impact, perception, and leverage
    

Your product exists in the gap between:

*   “What my manager _said_”
    
*   “What they _meant_”
    
*   “What I should _actually do differently_”
    

That gap is _huge_—and currently underserved.

3\. What Makes This a Product (Not Just a Smart Chatbot)
========================================================

The key differentiation is **persistent sense-making over time**.

### The product promise:

> “If you keep dumping your work thoughts here, I’ll help you see the patterns, decode the signals, and guide your decisions—like a mentor who never forgets.”

This only works if the system:

*   Remembers context
    
*   References prior conversations
    
*   Detects recurring themes
    
*   Evolves advice as the user evolves
    

Otherwise, it collapses into “smart journaling.”

4\. Mentor Behavior & Intelligence (Concrete Design)
====================================================

### 4.1 The mentor’s _default posture_

The mentor should **resist giving advice too early**.

Default flow:

1.  Reflect back what it heard (to build trust)
    
2.  Ask 2–5 _high-leverage clarifying questions_
    
3.  Identify the _category_ of problem
    
4.  Apply a framework
    
5.  Produce a concrete output
    
6.  Log the insight to memory
    

This mirrors how great Staff engineers and EMs actually think.

### 4.2 Structured thinking tools (how they show up in chat)

Not “here’s a framework”—but _applied quietly_.

Examples:

**5 Whys (implicit)**

> “When your manager says ‘be more proactive,’ what situations come to mind?”“What was the consequence of not acting sooner?”“Who noticed?”“What decision were they hoping you’d make without being asked?”

**First-principles**

> “If we strip away the wording, what outcome do you think your manager wants to see more of?”

**Trade-off analysis**

> “If you double down on technical depth this quarter, what might you be implicitly deprioritizing?”

**Reflection loops**

> “You’ve mentioned feeling ‘behind’ three times in the last month—do you think this is about skill gaps or visibility?”

### 4.3 Pattern detection (this is your moat)

Over time, the mentor can say things like:

*   “This is the third time feedback has involved cross-team communication.”
    
*   “You often get vague feedback when working on ambiguous projects.”
    
*   “You tend to interpret neutral feedback as negative—want to sanity-check that?”
    

This is _extremely_ powerful and hard to replicate without long-term memory.

5\. Example Use Cases (Fully Expanded)
======================================

Below I’ll outline **flows**, not just answers.

Use Case 1: “My manager said I need to be more impactful”
---------------------------------------------------------

### Mentor questioning strategy

1.  “What exact words did they use?”
    
2.  “In what context did they say this?”
    
3.  “What projects have you worked on recently?”
    
4.  “Who benefited from your work?”
    
5.  “What level are you at, and what level are you aiming for?”
    

### Frameworks used

*   Impact = **Value × Visibility × Leverage**
    
*   Level-based expectations
    
*   Outcome vs output distinction
    

### Output produced

**Impact Decode Summary**

*   What “impact” likely means _in this context_
    
*   2–3 plausible interpretations
    
*   Signals that would confirm each
    

**Action Plan**

*   One project to reframe around outcomes
    
*   One communication change
    
*   One stakeholder alignment habit
    

**Saved Insight**

> “Manager feedback theme: impact is being evaluated based on cross-team outcomes, not task completion.”

Use Case 2: “Help me decode this performance review”
----------------------------------------------------

### Mentor questioning strategy

*   “Which parts surprised you?”
    
*   “Which parts feel vague or unfair?”
    
*   “What evidence did they cite—or not cite?”
    
*   “How does this compare to previous feedback?”
    

### Frameworks used

*   Signal vs noise separation
    
*   Promotion rubric reverse-engineering
    
*   Feedback compression (what are they really saying?)
    

### Output produced

**Decoded Review**

*   Clear translation of each vague statement
    
*   What’s actionable vs political vs incomplete
    

**Clarifying Questions Draft**

*   What the user should ask their manager
    
*   How to ask without sounding defensive
    

**Memory Log**

> “Review pattern: feedback focuses on leadership behaviors, not technical execution.”

6\. Product Design & UX (Critical Decisions)
============================================

### 6.1 Ideal chat experience

*   Slack/iMessage-like
    
*   Calm, low-UI chrome
    
*   Encourages long messages
    
*   Explicit “brain dump mode”
    

Key UX detail:

> Make it _feel safe to be messy_.

### 6.2 Memory surfacing (do this carefully)

Good:

*   “This sounds similar to feedback you mentioned in October.”
    
*   “Last time, this came up when…”
    

Bad:

*   Overly confident assumptions
    
*   Excessive referencing (feels creepy)
    

Rule:

> Memory should feel like _helpful continuity_, not surveillance.

### 6.3 Insights, summaries, and revisiting

Core artifacts:

*   **Conversation summaries**
    
*   **Recurring themes**
    
*   **Goals**
    
*   **Action plans**
    

Let users:

*   Pin insights
    
*   Revisit decisions
    
*   See “what changed since then”
    

This is where the product becomes _compounding_.

### 6.4 Balancing emotional support vs strategy

You want:

*   Empathy **without** becoming therapy
    
*   Strategy **without** being robotic
    

Heuristic:

*   Validate emotions
    
*   Redirect to agency

8\. Risks & Blind Spots (Important)
===================================

### 1\. Over-advising too early

If the mentor gives answers too fast, users won’t trust it.

**Mitigation:** Force a questioning phase.

### 2\. Becoming “generic career advice”

If advice isn’t grounded in _their_ context, it loses credibility fast.

**Mitigation:** Aggressive context capture early.

### 3\. Emotional dependency risk

Some users may over-rely on the mentor.

**Mitigation:** Encourage real-world conversations and experiments.

### 4\. Trust & privacy

Engineers will dump _sensitive_ info.

**Mitigation:** Explicit privacy posture, optional local memory, transparency.
